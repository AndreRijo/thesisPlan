\section{Motivation and challenges}
A key insight is that in many large-scale services, especially when deployed world-wide,
data does not need to be replicated everywhere.
For instance, consider an e-commerce application with world-wide coverage.
The information regarding Portuguese customers, as well as stock of products in Portugal,
does not need to be replicated everywhere --- it is unlikely to be relevant outside of Europe.
Thus, it can be replicated in European data centers and, e.g., North American ones for fault tolerance purposes.
With this, we reduce the following costs: storage (each data center holds less data), network (updates of an object are only
sent to a subset of the data centers) and processing (fewer updates in total for each data center to process).
The method of replication in which each data center only replicates a subset of the system's data is known as
\emph{partial replication}.
However, partial replication is not a holy grail -- multiple issues arise when data is not replicated everywhere.

First, under partial replication, maintaining application properties (invariants) valid at all times is challenging.
For example, consider an invariant regarding sales of tickets for a concert across multiple sale spots: we may want to ensure that
we do not sell more tickets than the capacity of the venue.
The data regarding those sales may be spread across different data centers.
Depending on the criticality of the operation and the desired guarantees, different levels of consistency or coordination may be
required to preserve such invariants.
We return to data safety and invariants in more detail in Section~\ref{subsec:invariants}.%

Second, the replication algorithm running in each data center must know, for each
object it replicates, which other data centers also replicate that object.
This can be difficult to deal with, as the partitioning may change over time and the distribution of objects
may not be uniform across data centers -- that is, each data center may replicate different subsets of data, potentially
with overlaps.
For example, DC1 replicates objects \textit{A} and \textit{B}, while DC2 replicates \textit{A} but not \textit{B} and DC3 replicates \textit{B} but not \textit{A}.
Keeping an accurate and efficiently maintainable description of these replica sets is essential to avoid both unnecessary communication and missed updates.


Furthermore, supporting transactions under partial replication is challenging.
On one hand, a transaction may span objects that are all present in the origin data center, but whose replicas are spread across
different places in other data centers, as in the example above~\cite{spanner}.
A naïve solution would be to send the entire transaction to all data centers with at least one of those objects, but this would be
inefficient.
It is thus necessary to design an algorithm capable of analysing a transaction and sending only the necessary information
to each server, while still ensuring that data is kept consistent everywhere.
A suitable algorithm will both reduce the amount of data sent and the processing required on the receiving side.

Even with an optimal partitioning scheme, which is a difficult problem on its own, clients may sometimes issue transactions to
a data center where some operations are for objects not replicated locally~\cite{sipre}.
This poses many difficulties.
One could consider splitting a transaction and sending each part to one data center, but this may require coordination, increasing
latency.
It is also difficult to ensure consistency of data read in different places without further increasing latency.
Another solution would be to still execute the transaction as a whole, but have the data center starting the transaction
coordinate and fetch the necessary information in some form.
However, this still leads to the aforementioned problems.
In fact, if this kind of cross data center transactions are common, it may even be more practical to fall back to full replication.
Thus, a better alternative consists in creating mechanisms to reduce the frequency of such cross data center transactions.
Regardless, any system employing partial replication must explicitly address this issue.

The challenge above is further exacerbated when we consider complex recurring queries,
especially analytic or OLAP queries~\cite{dbtoaster,viewSelection,sioulas2023real}.
In analytic queries, often large amounts of data need to be processed~\cite{clickhouse, analyticdb}.
For instance, in an e-commerce system, one may want to query the ``top 10 most sold products world-wide''.
This kind of queries is essential to make well-informed business decisions~\cite{clickhouse, hadad}.
If a product suddenly becomes very popular, supply chains may need to be reinforced quickly to maintain stock, and it may
be wise to make the product easier to find in the application.
However, to compute this kind of information, a large amount of data needs to be processed --- potentially sales of all products.
Yet the query must be answered quickly and correctly; otherwise, the relevant business decision may happen too late,
leading to profit losses or negative impact on customer satisfaction.
This problem is already challenging under full replication, as it is difficult to process large volumes of data (long-executing
queries) while keeping results consistent and without hindering ongoing queries and updates~\cite{analyticdb}.
Partial replication complicates this problem further, as the necessary data is now spread across many different and faraway
data centers.
Proper mechanisms are essential, as coordinating long-executing queries across faraway data centers is not viable~\cite{clickhouse}.

In summary, partial replication is essential for cost-effective, large-scale geo-distributed applications, but it introduces significant technical challenges. Data is no longer uniformly available, complicating invariant preservation, transaction processing, replication coordination, and analytical query execution. In the following sections, we present our approach to addressing these challenges through a combination of novel replication algorithms, transaction protocols, and materialized view mechanisms, integrated into a practical geo-distributed database system called PotionDB.

\section{Proposed solution}

%In our work, we propose a geo-distributed database system that supports partial replication and addresses the challenges identified in the previous section.
%At a high level, our solution has three main pillars:
We propose PotionDB, a geo-distributed database system designed from the ground up to support partial replication efficiently. Our solution rests on three main pillars that directly address the challenges outlined above:
(i) a replication scheme tailored to partial (and possibly non-uniform) replication to reduce storage, network and processing costs;
(ii) a transactional layer that offers multiple consistency levels and supports invariants over partially replicated data; and
(iii) a materialized view subsystem that makes complex, recurring analytic queries efficient even when data is partitioned across many data centers.
These components are integrated into a full database prototype that we will use both as a proof-of-concept and as a platform for experimental evaluation.

The first pillar is a replication and partitioning scheme designed specifically for partial replication.
Instead of replicating every object everywhere, our system decides which data centers should hold each object, combining partial replication with ideas from non-uniform replication to further reduce costs.
The replication protocol must keep track of which data centers replicate each object, adapt to reconfiguration and rebalancing, and ensure that updates are propagated only where necessary.
By doing so, we aim to significantly reduce storage and network overhead, while still providing the redundancy required for fault tolerance and low latency.

The second pillar is a transactional and consistency layer that can operate correctly over this partially replicated state.
We plan to support interactive, read–write transactions that may touch objects whose replicas are distributed across different partitions and data centers.
Our design builds on weak and causal consistency models~\cite{dynamo, cops, walter, understandingEC, cure, saturn}, which are well suited for geo-distributed systems but are notoriously hard to program against due to exposed anomalies.
In our prototype, we will deploy causal consistency as the baseline model and extend it to handle both base data and materialized views.
On top of this, we intend to offer stronger guarantees when needed, so that applications can choose different consistency/latency trade-offs for different operations.
A central goal is to support application-level invariants (such as non-negative stock or capacity constraints) despite partial replication and weak consistency, by combining suitable consistency levels with invariant-preserving mechanisms.

The third pillar is a materialized view subsystem aimed at serving complex, recurring analytic queries efficiently.
These views summarize or pre-aggregate data spread across many partitions and data centers, so that queries such as ``top 10 most sold products world-wide'' can be answered quickly and without centralizing all raw data.
We plan to design algorithms that maintain these views incrementally and keep them up-to-date and consistent, even when the underlying base data is partially replicated.
This requires deciding how to place and replicate the views themselves, how to route updates efficiently, and how to avoid long-running cross–data center computations.
Efficiency of view maintenance is crucial, as materialized views are often seen as expensive to keep current in large-scale systems.

Finally, we will embody all these mechanisms in a practical geo-distributed database implementation.
The system will expose both a key–value interface and an SQL interface~\cite{sequel}, which must interoperate over the same underlying data.
This dual interface is important: key–value operations are well suited for low-level, latency-sensitive access, while SQL provides a more expressive and familiar interface for complex queries and application development.
Our aim is to make the underlying replication, transactional and view mechanisms as transparent as possible to application developers, while still allowing them to control the main performance and consistency trade-offs.

\section{Expected contributions}

Some of the goals outlined above have already been partially addressed, while others are part of ongoing or future work.
At the end of this PhD, we expect the following main contributions, which will be further developed in Chapter~\ref{cha:research_statement}:

\begin{itemize}
  \item The design and implementation of algorithms for efficient geo-distributed partial (and non-uniform) replication, reducing storage, network and processing costs compared to full replication, including mechanisms for version management and garbage collection, and support for dynamic re-partitioning of data;
  \item The design and implementation of a transaction protocol that supports multiple consistency levels over partially replicated data and can be combined with application-level invariants;
  \item A solution for analytic queries over large data volumes based on materialized views.
        Views may span data partitioned across many servers and data centers, and must remain up-to-date and consistent despite weak consistency and partial replication, with automatic and incremental view maintenance so that developers do not need to update views manually;
  \item The provision of useful correctness guarantees to application developers, through the combination of appropriate consistency models and explicit support for invariants under partial replication;
  \item A geo-distributed database prototype that implements the proposed algorithms and offers rich data types suitable for large-scale, geo-distributed applications;
  \item A rich database interface with support for both SQL and key–value access, providing operations appropriate for each data type and allowing applications to combine both styles in a single system.
\end{itemize}
