\chapter{Research Context}
\label{cha:research_context}

In this research work we propose a geo-replicated, weakly consistent database,
named PotionDB, which provides reliable access to complex analytical queries with
very low latency. Achieving this goal requires combining and building upon several
concepts and techniques from distributed systems and databases. Many alternative
solutions exist for each of these dimensions, so it is important to understand the
design space in order to make informed choices.

This chapter surveys the main concepts that underpin our work and positions
PotionDB within the state of the art. Section~\ref{sec:cap} recalls the CAP
theorem and explains how it shapes the design of distributed data stores.
Section~\ref{sec:consistency} reviews consistency models, with a focus on weak and
causal consistency. Section~\ref{sec:replication} discusses replication strategies,
including partial and non-uniform replication and their use in geo-distributed
settings. Finally, Section~\ref{sec:data-access} focuses on data access, queries,
and mechanisms to speed them up, and surveys existing systems that are most
closely related to PotionDB. Throughout the chapter we highlight the design
choices we adopt in PotionDB.

\section{CAP theorem}
\label{sec:cap}

In an ideal world, distributed systems would provide strongly consistent data, be
always available, and keep operating correctly even in the presence of failures and
network partitions. In practice, this is not possible. The CAP theorem~\cite{cap}
states that in the presence of network partitions, a distributed system can provide
at most two of the following properties:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \emph{Strong Consistency}: all replicas of a system present the same view of
        the data at any given time;
  \item \emph{Availability}: every request received by a non-failing node eventually
        receives a response;
  \item \emph{Partition tolerance}: the system continues to operate and evolve its
        state correctly despite message loss and network partitions.
\end{itemize}

Because network partitions are common in systems deployed across multiple
regions~\cite{bailis2013eventually}, partition tolerance is generally non-negotiable.
The practical design choice is therefore between maximizing strong consistency
or maximizing availability.

The CAP theorem should not be interpreted as forcing an all-or-nothing choice.
Systems that are classified as CP (Consistency + Partition tolerance) can still be
available in the presence of some failures, but will eventually stop accepting
operations if too many nodes fail or communication is severely disrupted in order
to avoid inconsistent states~\cite{bailis2013eventually}. Conversely, AP systems
(Availability + Partition tolerance) can still provide meaningful consistency
guarantees, often grouped under the umbrella of \emph{weak consistency}, but must
tolerate temporary divergence between replicas and may expose conflicting states
to applications~\cite{bailis2013eventually}.

PotionDB targets geo-distributed deployments where network partitions and high
latencies are unavoidable. We therefore embrace partition tolerance and high
availability, and build on weak consistency models that provide useful guarantees
while avoiding the coordination costs associated with strong consistency.

\section{Consistency}
\label{sec:consistency}

A wide range of consistency models has been proposed in the literature
(e.g.,~\cite{bernstein1987concurrency,linearizability,spanner,bailis2013eventually,occic2012causal,dynamo,session}).
Each model offers different guarantees on which states can be observed by clients,
and imposes different implementation costs and deployment restrictions. Despite
this variety, most models can be broadly classified as providing either some form
of \emph{strong} consistency or some form of \emph{weak} consistency.

\subsection{Strong consistency}
\label{sec:strong-consistency}

Under strong consistency, all clients observe the same state of the system
independently of which replica they contact. Achieving this generally requires a
total order on write operations, enforced via coordination among replicas
(e.g., consensus protocols or total order broadcast)~\cite{bernstein1987concurrency,linearizability,spanner,paxos,spanner}.
Depending on the system design, even read operations may need to contact a
majority of replicas or a designated master replica to ensure they observe the
latest committed state~\cite{spanner,si}.

These coordination requirements imply that strongly consistent systems must
compromise on availability in the presence of failures or partitions: to prevent
divergence or incorrect results, operations may have to block or be rejected when
enough replicas cannot be reached~\cite{bailis2013hatrack,bailis2013eventually}.
Moreover, in geo-distributed settings, the cost of coordination across distant
data centers can be prohibitively high; round-trip times quickly dominate
operation latency~\cite{spanner,bailis2013eventually,tilley2013cloud}
and can limit throughput under contention~\cite{bailis2013hatrack}.

On the positive side, strong consistency makes it easier to develop correct
applications: programmers can reason about a single, globally ordered sequence of
operations and are not exposed to many of the anomalies that appear under weaker
models~\cite{adya1999weak,avoidence}. Classical strong models include
serializability, linearizability and snapshot isolation~\cite{bernstein1987concurrency,linearizability,si}.
Recent work on strongly consistent systems has focused on reducing the number of
cross-replica round trips, exploiting data locality, and using batching and
pipelining to reduce commit latency and improve throughput~\cite{spanner, spanner,mu2014,wolf2016calvin}.

\subsection{Weak consistency}
\label{sec:weak-consistency}

Weak consistency models relax the requirements of strong consistency in exchange
for better availability, lower latency and higher scalability~\cite{bailis2013eventually,session,dynamo,occic2012causal}.
They allow replicas to diverge temporarily and may expose anomalies such as stale
reads, non-monotonic observations, or surprising re-orderings of operations.
Reasoning about application correctness becomes more challenging, since
concurrent updates to the same objects may lead to unintuitive results, and
clients may observe inconsistent intermediate states~\cite{adya1999weak,bailis2013eventually}.

A minimal and widely deployed model is \emph{eventual consistency}~\cite{bailis2013eventually,dynamo}.
It guarantees only that if updates stop, replicas will eventually converge to a
common state, but does not constrain which intermediate states can be observed
or how conflicts are resolved. This raises many questions: how are concurrent
updates to the same object handled; which values can a read return; and how does
state evolve while updates are being propagated?

To provide more structure, several stronger but still weak models have been
studied~\cite{bailis2013eventually,occic2012causal,session,adya1999weak,swiftcloud,lamport2019time}.
Among them, \emph{causal consistency} is particularly attractive. It guarantees
that operations that are causally related (in the sense of Lamport’s happened-before
relation~\cite{lamport2019time}) are seen in a compatible order by all clients:
for example, a read that happens after a write must reflect the effects of that
write~\cite{occic2012causal,bailis2013eventually}. This avoids anomalies such as
seeing a reply to a message before seeing the message itself in a group
communication system.

Causal consistency can be enforced at the level of individual objects or across
multiple objects~\cite{bailis2013eventually}. Cross-object causality is more
demanding, as dependencies between updates to different objects must be tracked,
but greatly simplifies application logic. For instance, in an online shopping
system where users subscribe to restock notifications, we want the notification
to be causally ordered after the restocking operation so that users do not see a
notification before the product is actually available.

Causal+ consistency extends causal consistency by guaranteeing that replicas
eventually converge by resolving conflicts deterministically in all replicas~\cite{occic2012causal,bailis2013eventually}.
Other useful weak models include monotonic reads~\cite{session}, which
ensure that consecutive reads of the same object never move backwards in time,
and snapshot-based models such as snapshot isolation (SI) and its variants,
which provide a consistent snapshot per transaction but still allow certain
anomalies~\cite{si,yu2000practical}.

Snapshot Isolation (SI) offers each transaction a consistent snapshot of the
database: reads never block and always see a self-consistent state, and write
conflicts are detected at commit time, requiring coordination and a total order
on committing transactions~\cite{si}. Parallel Snapshot Isolation
(PSI) relaxes this by allowing transactions in different replicas to commit in
different orders~\cite{walter}. PSI is attractive for geo-distributed systems, as it
can provide strong guarantees for transactions that access a single replica,
while still allowing high throughput and low latency in many cases; however,
coordination is still required in some scenarios.

\paragraph{Consistency in PotionDB.}

PotionDB builds on these ideas by providing PSI extended with commutative sets
(csets), as proposed in Walter~\cite{walter}, together with causal+ consistency for
transactions that span different servers. Causality is tracked across objects and
across servers, so that when a client observes an update to an object, it also
observes the corresponding updates to related objects and views.

Because our data types are implemented as CRDTs (see
Section~\ref{sec:crdts}), all update operations are commutative. This eliminates
write conflicts between concurrent transactions and allows transactions to commit
without synchronization across data centers. To support use cases where even
higher throughput is desirable and full causal ordering is unnecessary, PotionDB
also supports monotonic reads for clients that remain sticky to a single server.
Even in this mode, clients only observe states produced by committed
transactions.

\subsection{CRDTs and other conflict resolution techniques}
\label{sec:crdts}

In weakly consistent systems, concurrent updates to the same objects are common
and can lead to conflicts~\cite{bailis2013eventually}. For example, if an element
is concurrently added to and removed from a set, or if multiple writes to a
register occur at the same time, the final state is not obvious. To ensure that
replicas converge, conflicts must be resolved deterministically.

Several approaches exist. Conflicts can be prevented through coordination (e.g.,
using strong consistency), operations can be totally ordered using logical clocks
and replica identifiers, or conflicting versions can be handed to the
application for resolution. A more scalable and developer-friendly approach is
to design the data types themselves so that all relevant operations commute.

PotionDB adopts CRDTs (Conflict-free Replicated Data Types)~\cite{crdt}
for its core data types. CRDTs are designed for large-scale, weakly consistent
systems: they guarantee that, when all updates have been delivered, all replicas
will converge to the same state, even under arbitrary re-orderings and
duplications of messages. Updates and reads can be served locally without
synchronization, enabling high availability and low latency; updates are
propagated asynchronously in the background~\cite{crdt,stateCRDT,crdt-caching}.

CRDTs come in two main flavours: state-based and operation-based. In state-based
CRDTs, replicas periodically ship their state (or deltas) and merge states using
a join operation on a monotonic semi-lattice; in operation-based CRDTs, replicas
propagate the effect of operations and rely on commutativity and appropriate
delivery guarantees~\cite{crdt,stateCRDT}. Multiple
policies can be supported for the same abstract type (e.g., different semantics
for sets), increasing flexibility~\cite{crdt}.

Other commutativity-based approaches exist. RADTs (Replicated Abstract Data
Types)~\cite{preguicarpadt} share similarities with CRDTs but offer fewer
conflict-resolution policies. Operational Transformation (OT)~\cite{ot,otCorrectness}
transforms operations upon delivery so that they commute, but designing correct
transformations is often more complex and error-prone than designing CRDT
operations to be commutative from the outset~\cite{crdt}. We
therefore base PotionDB on CRDTs, leveraging their well-understood semantics and
rich toolbox of existing designs.

\section{Replication}
\label{sec:replication}

Replication is central to distributed systems: it improves availability, fault
tolerance and latency, but also increases storage, network and processing
costs~\cite{abbadi1990faulttolerant,dynamo}. The choice of replication
strategy must take into account the consistency model, failure assumptions,
access patterns, and deployment (e.g., within a single data center or across
continents). In PotionDB we combine several replication concepts to obtain a
model tailored to our geo-distributed, partially replicated setting.

\subsection{Synchronous and asynchronous replication}

In \emph{synchronous} replication, an update is considered complete only after it
has been replicated to and acknowledged by a subset of replicas (often a
majority)~\cite{tanenbaum2006distributed}. This is common in strongly consistent
systems, which rely on protocols such as Paxos or state-machine replication to
totally order operations and ensure that replicas remain up-to-date and do not
diverge~\cite{paxos,schneider1990implementing,spanner}. While this
provides strong guarantees, it increases operation latency—especially when
replicas are far apart—and limits availability, since operations may block or
fail if enough replicas cannot be contacted~\cite{spanner,bailis2013eventually}.

In \emph{asynchronous} replication, an update may be confirmed locally before it
is propagated to other replicas~\cite{tanenbaum2006distributed}. This is a natural
fit for weakly consistent systems, allowing operations to complete with low
latency and enabling higher throughput. Replication can be performed in the
background, possibly batching multiple updates for efficiency~\cite{dynamo,occic2012causal}.
The downside is that replicas can diverge and conflicts must be handled, as
discussed previously.

PotionDB adopts asynchronous replication, which aligns with its weak consistency
model and geo-distributed setting: avoiding cross–data center coordination on
the critical path is essential for low latency and high availability.

\subsection{Operation- and state-based replication}

An important design choice is how to propagate changes to replicas. Two broad
approaches are commonly distinguished~\cite{crdt}:

\begin{description}
  \item[State-based replication.] Replicas periodically send their entire state,
        or a compact representation of it, to other replicas, which merge it with
        their local state. If merges are monotonic and idempotent, and all
        replicas eventually receive sufficiently recent states, convergence is
        guaranteed.
  \item[Operation-based replication.] Replicas propagate the operations (or
        their effects) that were executed locally. Receiving replicas apply these
        operations to their local state, subject to ordering and delivery
        guarantees required by the consistency model.
\end{description}

Both approaches have advantages and limitations. Operation-based replication can
lead to many small messages when updates are frequent, whereas state-based
replication may be too expensive for large objects, as even small changes require
sending large states. Optimizations exist in both cases: operations can be
batched, and state-based approaches can send only deltas between states, as in
delta-CRDTs~\cite{stateCRDT,deltaAlmeida}.

PotionDB uses operation-based replication for its CRDT objects, which fits well
with asynchronous, causally ordered communication and allows us to exploit the
commutativity of CRDT updates.

\subsection{Partial replication}
\label{subsec:partial}

Many distributed storage systems employ full replication: every data center
replicates the entire dataset~\cite{dynamo,cure}. This simplifies
system design and can provide high fault tolerance and low read latency, but it
does not scale well as the number of data centers grows. Storage, network and
processing costs increase linearly with the number of replicas, since every
update must be processed everywhere~\cite{bailis2013eventually,zhang2013towards}.

Two complementary mechanisms are used to mitigate these costs:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \emph{Internal partitioning (sharding)} divides the dataset across
        shards within a data center, allowing the system to exploit multi-core
        hardware and multiple servers and to scale to datasets that no longer
        fit in a single machine~\cite{dynamo,bailis2013eventually}.
  \item \emph{External partitioning (partial replication)} assigns different
        subsets of the dataset to different data centers. Each data center
        stores only a fraction of the objects, often chosen based on data
        locality or other application-specific criteria~\cite{spanner,walter,zhang2013towards}.
\end{itemize}

Partial replication is especially attractive for geo-distributed services with
strong locality patterns~\cite{walter,zhang2013towards}. For example, in an
international e-commerce application, data about European customers and sales is
rarely needed in Asia. Replicating it only in European data centers (plus a few
others for fault tolerance) reduces storage and update costs significantly.
Moreover, transactions that access only local data need to coordinate with fewer
sites, which reduces latency and increases throughput.

However, partial replication raises new challenges. Because no data center holds
the full dataset, partitioning must be chosen so that most transactions access
data within a single data center; otherwise transactions frequently need to
coordinate across data centers, even under weak consistency, which hurts
throughput and latency~\cite{walter,spanner,zhang2013towards}. Partitioning
schemes may need to adapt over time as access patterns change, requiring
efficient mechanisms for migrating data between data centers~\cite{zhang2013towards,irwin2010fly}.

PotionDB embraces partial replication to keep replication costs manageable
as the number of data centers grows, while carefully designing its transactional
and view mechanisms to minimize cross–data center coordination.

\subsection{Geo replication}

Large-scale Internet services often serve users worldwide and therefore deploy
data centers across multiple continents. We refer to systems that replicate data
across distant data centers as \emph{geo-replicated}. Geo replication is
challenging due to high and variable latencies and limited bandwidth between
data centers~\cite{spanner,bailis2013eventually}. Combining strong
consistency with high availability in such environments is particularly
difficult, as already discussed in Section~\ref{sec:cap}.

In wide-area replication within a single region (e.g., across data centers in
the same country), cross–data center coordination may still be acceptable, and
several systems exploit this~\cite{spanner,occic2012causal}. In truly
geo-distributed deployments, however, round-trip times can easily reach hundreds
of milliseconds. Systems that rely on multiple cross–data center RTTs per
operation often incur latencies that are intolerable for interactive
applications~\cite{bailis2013eventually,spanner}.

Some systems provide strong consistency in geo-replicated settings, but at the
cost of high latency, limited availability and often reduced
throughput~\cite{spanner,mdcc,slog,detock}. Other designs offer strong
consistency within a data center but weaker guarantees across data
centers~\cite{occic2012causal,cops,eiger}. Weakly consistent systems that provide
eventual or causal consistency are generally better suited for geo replication,
but they shift some of the burden of dealing with anomalies to application
developers~\cite{dynamo,occic2012causal,bailis2013eventually}.

PotionDB is designed from the outset for geo replication under weak consistency.
It uses asynchronous, operation-based replication, partial replication and
CRDTs to avoid placing geo-distributed coordination on the critical path of most
operations.

\subsection{Non-uniform replication}
\label{subsec:nonuniform}

Partial replication reduces costs by storing fewer replicas of each object, but
does not address the case of large or highly updated objects. In many
applications, only a small portion of an object’s state is relevant for reads.
For instance, a global “top 10 most sold products” leaderboard in a large
e-commerce system may receive updates for hundreds of thousands of products, but
only the very top entries matter for reads; the vast majority of updates do not
change the visible state.

\emph{Non-uniform replication}~\cite{nonUniform} exploits this by
replicating only the part of the state that affects the visible behaviour of an
object. Updates that may become relevant in the future are stored locally and
maybe partially replicated for fault tolerance, while updates that will never
affect the visible state can be discarded. This reduces storage, network and
processing overheads for large objects while preserving read correctness.

Cabrita et al.~\cite{nonUniform,nonUniformThesis} propose several
non-uniform CRDTs, including Top-K and Top-Sum leaderboards, histograms and
filtered sets. A related notion is \emph{computational CRDTs}~\cite{computationalCrdt},
in which applications are interested only in the result of a computation over the
state (e.g., an average) rather than in the full state itself. These CRDTs keep
only the minimal information required to compute the desired result, further
reducing storage and communication costs.

PotionDB incorporates non-uniform and computational CRDTs to support efficient
global statistics and analytic queries, as discussed in Chapter~\ref{cha:research_statement}.

\subsection{Replication in PotionDB}

PotionDB is a geo-replicated database that supports both full and partial
asynchronous replication. For each group of objects, system administrators can
choose whether it is fully or partially replicated and in which data centers it
should reside. Replication is operation-based and leverages CRDT semantics to
guarantee convergence.

Within each server, PotionDB shards data across worker threads to exploit
multi-core CPUs. Across servers and data centers, it combines partial
replication with non-uniform and computational CRDTs, including new designs
(e.g., max/min CRDTs) and extensions (such as a Top-Sum CRDT with decrements).
We return to the details and motivation for these choices in
Chapter~\ref{cha:research_statement}.

\section{Data access}
\label{sec:data-access}

Online services rely on storage systems to manage their data. Databases provide
different guarantees (e.g., consistency, durability) and different kinds of
interfaces for accessing and manipulating data. While many variants exist, two
common abstractions are key–value stores and relational databases. Most systems
can be understood as variants or combinations of these.

\paragraph{Key–value stores.}

Key–value stores provide a simple interface, typically with two main operations:
\texttt{get} and \texttt{put}~\cite{dynamo,occic2012causal,cure,walter,redis}.
Objects are identified by keys and are often opaque values, although many systems
support richer data types such as counters, sets or maps, and some offer
transactions~\cite{cure,walter,redis}. PotionDB follows this model, but extends
it with a wide range of CRDTs and transactional operations, including access to
materialized views.

\paragraph{Relational databases.}

Relational databases organize data into tables with rows (tuples) and columns,
and support relationships between tables~\cite{silberschatz2005database}.
Structured Query Language (SQL) or SQL-like languages~\cite{sequel,spanner} are
used to define schemas and query data. SQL offers a rich declarative interface
that greatly simplifies application development. For example, a single SQL query
can compute the average salary of employees in a given department, whereas a
simple key–value store would require fetching multiple objects and computing the
average in application code, or maintaining a separate pre-computed result.

Key–value stores typically emphasize high performance and availability, often
under weaker consistency models~\cite{dynamo,occic2012causal,cure},
while relational databases emphasize expressive queries and stronger
consistency, at the cost of more complex implementations and potentially higher
latency~\cite{silberschatz2005database,spanner}. Some systems attempt to bridge
the gap by adding richer data types or limited transaction support to key–value
stores~\cite{cure,walter}.

\subsection{Queries}

Applications access data in many ways. Some mostly perform simple lookups, such
as fetching information about a specific product. Others require summaries,
aggregations, sorting or more complex processing across large datasets.

Online Analytical Processing (OLAP) refers to techniques for analyzing and
organizing data using analytical queries~\cite{olap,olap2}. Such queries are
commonly used for marketing, financial reporting, business intelligence and
scientific analysis. In an e-commerce system, for example, it is important to
track trending products to adjust supply, pricing or advertising. This is
challenging: very large volumes of data must be analyzed (all relevant sales),
the data is continuously updated, and results must be produced quickly enough to
support timely decisions.

Common approaches to support analytical queries include materialized views,
indexes, windowed computations and caches~\cite{olap,olap2,dbtoaster,analyticdb}.
Operations such as joins, aggregations (sums, averages), sorting, limits and
grouping are heavily used. Existing solutions often require expensive
infrastructures, long-running queries, or accept stale or inconsistent data in
order to keep costs acceptable~\cite{analyticdb,dbtoaster,sioulas2023real}.

The TPC-H benchmark~\cite{tpch,dbtoaster,analyticdb} exemplifies a sales-oriented
database that supports complex queries for decision making. Its query workload
captures many of the characteristics of real-world analytical workloads that we
intend to support efficiently in PotionDB.

\subsection{Speeding up queries: views and indexes}

To speed up data access and simplify application development, systems employ
various mechanisms in addition to direct access, including indexes, views,
materialized views, streaming operators and caches~\cite{silberschatz2005database,olap,dbtoaster,analyticdb}.
Here we briefly review indexes and views.

\paragraph{Indexes.}

An index is an auxiliary data structure that accelerates access to records based
on some key or predicate~\cite{silberschatz2005database,graefe2011modern}. In relational
databases, indexes can be created on columns to support fast lookups, grouping or
sorting by those columns. In non-relational systems, maps or other structures
can play a similar role, mapping keys to object identifiers. Indexes are widely
used in both OLTP and OLAP workloads to speed up queries.

\paragraph{Views and materialized views.}

A view is a virtual table defined by a query over base tables~\cite{silberschatz2005database}.
Views are often used for abstraction, security and reuse of complex subqueries.
They are recomputed on demand when queried and do not, by themselves, improve
performance.

\emph{Materialized views} cache the results of a query, storing them physically
to allow faster access~\cite{silberschatz2005database,dbtoaster,analyticdb}. They can greatly
reduce the cost of recurring queries by avoiding repeated execution of expensive
operations. However, they introduce challenges: extra storage is required, and
views must be kept reasonably up-to-date and consistent with base data. Maintaining
materialized views incrementally and efficiently is nontrivial, especially in
the presence of frequent updates, distributed data and weak
consistency~\cite{dbtoaster,dbsp,analyticdb}.

View maintenance remains an active research area~\cite{dbtoaster,dbsp,analyticdb}.
Systems such as DBToaster~\cite{dbtoaster} and DBSP~\cite{dbsp} explore different
approaches to incremental maintenance and streaming semantics, but typically
assume centralized or strongly consistent settings and full data availability,
which limits their applicability to geo-replicated, partially replicated systems.

\subsection{Data safety}
\label{sec:data-safety}

Many applications require certain conditions (invariants) to hold at all
times. Examples include non-negative account balances in banking, capacity
constraints in ticketing systems, or uniqueness of identifiers~\cite{avoidence,ipa}.
Under strong consistency, invariants can often be enforced by checking
conditions before performing updates, but this is burdensome and error-prone for
developers, especially when many invariants interact.

Under weak consistency, maintaining invariants is more challenging. Concurrent
operations may each observe a state that appears valid locally but jointly
violate the invariant. For instance, two concurrent withdrawals of 10 units from
an account with balance 15 may both succeed, temporarily exposing a negative
balance.

A substantial body of work has studied how to detect conflicting operations and
enforce invariants while minimizing coordination~\cite{avoidence,ipa,burckhardt2014principles,novakovic2013owntheconsistency,escrow,escrow2,no-op}.
Traditional approaches often fall back to strong consistency for operations that
might conflict. More recent work explores invariant-preserving techniques under
weak consistency, such as escrow-based methods for numeric invariants~\cite{escrow,escrow2},
invariant-preserving abstractions such as IPA and Antidote SQL~\cite{ipa,antidotesql},
and frameworks that disable or adjust conflicting operations (e.g., No-Op~\cite{no-op}).
Numeric and aggregation invariants remain particularly challenging; some
approaches rely on escrow, while others restrict concurrency significantly.

PotionDB aims to provide invariant support in a weakly consistent, partially
replicated setting by combining consistency levels with CRDT-based techniques.
We revisit this topic in Chapter~\ref{cha:research_statement}.

\subsection{Existing systems}
\label{sec:existing-systems}

We now review a selection of systems that are particularly relevant to PotionDB,
either because they target geo replication, support partial replication, provide
weak consistency with transactions, or offer advanced support for analytical
queries.

\paragraph{Spanner.}

Spanner~\cite{spanner} is Google’s globally distributed, strongly consistent
database. It supports multi-version data, external (geo) replication and
(partial) data placement. Spanner’s timestamps rely on Google’s TrueTime API,
which combines GPS and atomic clocks to provide bounded clock uncertainty and
enable lock-free snapshot reads. Replication is based on Paxos, and data is
sharded across Paxos groups. Cross-shard transactions require two-phase commit,
which increases latency and limits fault tolerance. Spanner’s reliance on tight
clock synchronization and cross–data center coordination makes it challenging to
use in scenarios requiring very low latency across distant regions.

\paragraph{MDCC.}

MDCC~\cite{mdcc} is a geo-distributed, strongly consistent system that aims to
commit transactions in a single wide-area RTT by combining several Paxos
variants and exploiting operation commutativity. While single RTT commits are
attractive, MDCC still requires at least one RTT for any transaction, and
non-commutative updates may require multiple RTTs, which can be unacceptable for
latency-sensitive applications. MDCC does not support partial replication.

\paragraph{SLOG, Caerus and Detock.}

SLOG~\cite{slog} is a strongly consistent geo-replicated system that leverages
data locality: each item has a master data center, and transactions that access
only items mastered in the same data center can commit without cross–data center
coordination. This yields low latency when access patterns match master
placement, but leads to high latency when clients are far from the masters or
transactions span multiple masters.

Caerus and Detock~\cite{caerus,detock} also aim for single-RTT cross–data center
commits by relying on deterministic transactions and carefully chosen replication
and commit protocols. They obtain impressive latencies in some scenarios, but
sacrifice fault tolerance (e.g., limiting replication within a region) and
assume that read/write sets of transactions are known in advance, which is not
always realistic.

\paragraph{Dynamo.}

Dynamo~\cite{dynamo} is Amazon’s eventually consistent key–value
store. It uses a zero-hop DHT for partitioning and replicates each object on a
configurable number of nodes. Reads and writes contact configurable quorums of
replicas, allowing different performance and durability trade-offs. Dynamo
returns conflicting versions to applications, leaving conflict resolution to
developers. Its emphasis on availability and low latency has informed many
subsequent systems, but the lack of structured conflict resolution and richer
data types makes application development harder.

\paragraph{COPS and Eiger.}

COPS~\cite{cops} is a wide-area key–value store that provides causal+ consistency
across data centers. Data is sharded within each data center, and causal
dependencies between objects are tracked explicitly. COPS-GT extends COPS with
read-only transactions. Achieving these guarantees requires nontrivial metadata
and garbage-collection mechanisms; COPS-GT can suffer reduced throughput and
unbounded metadata growth under partitions. Eiger~\cite{eiger} extends COPS with
write and read–write transactions and richer datatypes, but neither system
supports partial replication across data centers.

\paragraph{Cure.}

Cure~\cite{cure} is a geo-replicated key–value store that provides highly
available, causally consistent read–write transactions over CRDT-based data
types. It employs optimizations to ensure non-blocking reads and monotonic
snapshots even in the presence of failures. Cure offers a rich interface and
automatic conflict resolution, but does not support partial replication or
materialized views. PotionDB builds upon ideas from Cure, extending them with
partial replication, non-uniform replication and advanced support for
materialized views and analytical queries.

\paragraph{Walter.}

Walter~\cite{walter} is a geo-replicated key–value store that provides partial
replication and PSI consistency. It introduces preferred sites, where writes to
an object are more efficient, and counting sets (csets), which ensure that
operations on certain sets commute. Walter demonstrates the benefits of partial
replication and PSI, but does not focus on complex analytical workloads or
materialized views.

\paragraph{Systems for materialized views and incremental maintenance.}

Several systems focus specifically on efficient maintenance of materialized
views and streaming queries, such as DBToaster~\cite{dbtoaster}, DBSP~\cite{dbsp}
and various analytical databases~\cite{analyticdb,clickhouse}. DBToaster
generates incremental view-maintenance triggers that aim for (amortized)
constant-time updates per base update. DBSP provides a general model for
incremental maintenance over streams of database snapshots. These systems show
that complex queries can be maintained efficiently, but typically assume
centralized or strongly consistent settings and full data availability, which
limits their applicability in geo-replicated, partially replicated scenarios.

\subsection{Data access in PotionDB}

PotionDB adopts a key–value interface extended with rich CRDT-based data types.
It exposes multiple read and update operations tailored to each CRDT, including
operations that access partial state (e.g., membership queries on sets).

On top of this, PotionDB provides highly available, causally consistent
transactions with PSI+cset semantics, as discussed in
Section~\ref{sec:weak-consistency}. This combination allows developers to
perform transactional updates over partially replicated CRDTs while benefiting
from weak-consistency performance and availability.

A central goal of PotionDB is to support recurring analytical queries with
latencies comparable to simple reads on base objects. Analytical and OLAP-style
queries are typically recurring (e.g., “top 10 most sold products”), benefit
from low latency to enable timely decisions, and should not significantly degrade
the throughput or latency of other operations. PotionDB addresses this by
supporting materialized views that are automatically and incrementally
maintained, even when the underlying data is partitioned across multiple data
centers. These mechanisms are presented in detail in
Chapter~\ref{cha:research_statement}.
